Background of the Project

Queueing in Theme Parks

A Historic Overview

Early theme parks were incredibly simple, with few thrill rides that usually weren’t themed. Walt Disney’s creation of Disneyland completely changed the idea of theme parks, turning them into immersive lands prioritizing storytelling and theming alongside rides. Later in the 20th century, theme parks started utilizing intellectual property (IP) from film, television, and comics. The first majorly successful use of IP was the Wizarding World of Harry Potter in Universal Studios. At this point, the gold standard of theme parks became actually having visitors participate within the story of the park. Now, many theme parks utilize advanced technology like augmented reality (AR), virtual reality (VR), and projection mapping, and theme park areas react to guests in real time, through software like MagicBands or mobile apps. The Evolution of Theme Parks: From Fantasy to Hyper-Reality argues that the future of theme parks is heading towards hyper-reality, blending real world stimuli with digitally enhanced perception (2025).

Immersive Theming

The Evolution of Theme Parks: From Fantasy to Hyper-Reality argues that the future of theme parks is heading towards hyper-reality, blending real world stimuli with digitally enhanced perception (2025). According to the study Theme Park Queue Line Perception by Daniels et al.., there is some significance between design efforts within queues and a visitor’s perceived wait time (2017). However, the study also found a relationship between time of day and guest perception that suggests that as the day goes on, people perceive longer wait times (Daniels et al., 2017). Enhancing queues to reduce perceived wait time in this way is known as suspended reality (Daniels et al., 2017). A lot of thought and effort is put into suspended reality, and everything from smell, material, color, lighting, and sound can make an impression on a visitor (Spatial Design Principles of Immersive Ride Layouts in Amusement Parks, 2026). The goal is to provide a gradual change from the outside of the park into the ride while keeping the theming constant, while almost making the visitor feel as though they are entering another world (Spatial Design Principles of Immersive Ride Layouts in Amusement Parks, 2026).

Queues in Theme Parks

Queue systems, and good queue management are incredibly important within theme parks, not just for visitor satisfaction and enjoyment, but also for visitor safety. There have been many examples in history, such as soccer games or concerts, where crowd crushes resulting from overcrowding or tight spaces have caused the deaths of multiple people. In a large crowd, people become liquid and move like liquid . For this reason, the shape of a queue becomes incredibly important. The most simple type of queue is known as a serpentine queue, with lines of people in parallel rows. This becomes inconvenient in theme parks, where many people are being pulled out of the line at one time. To solve this issue, many rides have pre-show waiting areas. While providing entertainment for guests, it allows employees to group people together in the right sized mass, allowing the ride to be loaded faster and reducing overall wait time (Niles, 2025). In a large crowd with a gap to exit or board a ride that is too narrow, the flow of people slows down dramatically and can even become dangerous. Therefore, many rides with large queues will have a U-turn right before the loading area. This serves as a funnel, therefore narrowing the flow of people into a single file. However, there is only so much a park can do to reduce the actual wait time, considering the massive influx of people that may enter the park each day.

Express Passes & Lightning Lanes

While queues are thoughtfully managed to reduce actual and perceived wait time, the sheer load of visitors within a park means that the average person might still be waiting many hours for popular rides. This is why special entry tickets, such as express passes in Universal Orlando or Disney’s lightning lane. These tickets allow guests to bypass the regular queue line. In Orlando, Express passes can be either basic, which allows visitors to skip the regular queue once per ride per day, or unlimited, which allows visitors to skip the regular queue for any participating ride as many times as they like. This also means that visitors will usually skip out on some of the immersive theming and storytelling within the queue. Since express passes are very limited, the express queue will usually be a very short line. Employees will load visitors on the ride, keeping some ratio of express to regular admission guests (for example, for every 3 express guests loaded on to the ride, 1 regular admission guest is loaded). This ratio depends heavily on the popularity of the ride and the crowd level at that moment. At Disney, the lightning lane is also an express pass for purchase similar to the express pass; it allows a visitor to bypass the regular queue and enter a shorter queue for a popular attraction. However, Disney does not have unlimited passes; instead, they have multi passes, which allow visitors to bypass the queue on 3 attractions, single use passes, and premier passes, which allow visitors to bypass the queue one per ride per day. Tickets that allow guests to bypass the queue are both expensive and limited, which presents a new monetary barrier to enjoyment within the parks.

However, most of the ways a visitor can reduce waiting time are an additional, high charge on top of regular admission. This could create a monetary barrier to visitor enjoyment within a theme park. By building a forecasting plot, regular admission guests could anticipate when the queues of their favorite attractions will be least full during the day, and they would be able to reduce the amount of time spent waiting in lines.

This study will investigate theme park queues through discussing queueing theory, discrete event simulation, perceived versus actual wait time, and more, and attempt to provide a solution for visitors to spend less time queuing for attractions.

Queueing theory is the mathematical analysis of how stuff moves through a system, and it combines probability (the study of typical issues of chance) and optimization (the study of the exception for issues of choice (Ledder, 2019). A queue system has a bank of parallel service channels with a stream of customers entering at distinct times and receiving service (Ledder, 2019). Queuing systems can be defined through different elements and parameters, such as the probability distribution of the arrival or service process (Ledder, 2019). There are two ways to measure time within a queueing system: actual wait time and perceived wait time. Efficient queue management reduces actual wait time and enhances perceived wait time (AB, n.d.). Common queueing systems are paper tickets with call up displays, appointment booking, self-service kiosks, and virtual queuing systems (AB, n.d.). When a queueing system is modeled as a sequence of discrete events, it is known as discrete event simulation (Autodesk, 2023). The system is broken down into entities (objects moving through the system), events (interactions or changes that happen), and resources (elements required to perform events) (Autodesk, 2023). Within discrete event simulation, queues order entities and sort them, and servers delay entities until certain conditions are met (Autodesk, 2023). Queues can behave in different ways; they can have different capacities, sorting policies, and overwriting policies (what happens when the queue is full) (Autodesk, 2023). Two common sorting policies are first-in-first-out (FIFO) or last-in-first-out (LIFO) (Overview of Queues and Servers in Discrete-Event Simulation - MATLAB & Simulink, 2026). The simulation tracks the state of the system over time and provides details about the performance of the system under different conditions (Autodesk, 2023). While many sources highlight the role and importance of queueing systems and discrete event simulation in processes like manufacturing, there is little information about what specific queueing systems are used within theme parks or attractions, and how these systems might be organized.

However, if there was a way for regular admission guests to anticipate when the queues of their favorite attractions will be least full during the day, they would be able to reduce the amount of time spent waiting in lines. This can be done through predictive modeling, or using established data to forecast future outcomes. There are many types of predictive modelling; for the purposes of a predictive model on waiting times for theme park queues, time series analysis would work best. Time series analysis records and analyzes data points at regular time intervals, therefore showing how data changes over time (10 Types of Predictive Modeling: Benefits and Practical Applications, 2026).These models can show trends, seasonality, cyclical variations, and irregularity in data over time (10 Types of Predictive Modeling: Benefits and Practical Applications, 2026). This type of model should be used when historical data is consistent and reliable, temporal patterns or trends exist in the data, or future behavior depends on past observations (GeeksforGeeks, 2024). It should not be used if data is highly noisy, there are no meaningful historical patterns, or key influencing factors are unknown/unpredictable (GeeksforGeeks, 2024). While collecting the data, it will be important to investigate any possible influencing factors or confounding variables, such as weather, as well as whether the data shows a temporal pattern or trend. A univariate model, which records one variable over time, can be used to track queue waiting times over time (GeeksforGeeks, 2024). This model would also be discrete, collecting data at fixed intervals of time, and it may be continuous, or having changing patterns like trends or seasonality (GeeksforGeeks, 2024). A Holt-Winters exponential smoothing model is best suited for this task, as it can analyze data that shows a certain temporal trend or seasonality (GeeksforGeeks, 2024).

Most of the precedent for using time series analysis is related to traffic forecasting, weather forecasting, retail sales, finance and stock prediction, and product demand forecasting, not for theme parks. Many crowd calendars/crowd forecasts, which measure how busy the crowd is at a park on a certain day, can be found online. The website Queue Times shows official wait times for rides in theme parks all around the world (Bull, 2026). Alongside this, it shows visitors a crowd forecast; a person can look over the next week, month, or jump to another date to see the crowd forecast. It also provides average crowd level by month for different parks, and park attendance by year. The website does not predict ride wait times. It does give historical data for ride wait times, which will give plenty of data to find trends and/or seasonality when building a predictive model. While it does not use predictive modeling for ride wait times, an article from NOLA gives an approach to using time series analysis to predict queue wait times, but it is geared towards the operator/manager of a theme park, and does not go into significant detail on actually building the model, instead focusing on best practices (Queue Wait Time Analytics in Theme Parks, 2024). Another study, Predicting the Expected Waiting Time of Popular Attractions in Walt Disney World, actually builds a predictive model for expected wait times for rides in Walt Disney World, however it focuses on predicting the average wait time per day instead of wait time during the day. and the data, collected from 2012-2018, is not very recent (Mendoza et al., n.d.). This study hopes to delve further into this topic, and build a predictive model that will be more useful to visitors of the park.

Through the rise of immersive theming, IP, and interactive play, theme parks have become hotspots of vacation for people all over the world. For example, about 21 million people visit Universal Studios Orlando annually (Universal Orlando Tourism Statistics - How Many People Visit?, 2023). The increasing popularity of theme parks as a vacation destination now brings up the question of accessibility and equity. In a world where few people can afford expensive express tickets, many regular entry visitors find themselves paying hundreds of dollars to wait in lines as long as three or four hours. A person’s enjoyment of the park that they paid to enter should not be blocked behind monetary barriers.What if there was a way for people without express passes to know what time it would be best to visit their favorite attractions and plan their day accordingly. A predictive model that finds the best time to visit an attraction, when the queue will be the most empty, could help with this task.
